Polish Media Giant Agora Cuts 166 Jobs

Published: 2025-12-30

Agora announces 166 job cuts across Wyborcza, Gazeta.pl, and radio units as digital transformation falters. Poland's largest media group cites ineffective digital model, automation push as 25.8% of Gazeta.pl staff face layoffs.

Poland's Media Powerhouse Restructures

Agora, one of Poland's largest media conglomerates, delivered unwelcome news on December 30, 2025: up to 166 employees will lose their jobs as the company restructures its struggling digital operations. The layoffs represent 6.56% of the group's total workforce and will proceed through February 2026 across multiple subsidiaries.

The boards of three Agora subsidiaries — Wyborcza sp. z o.o., Gazeta.pl sp. z o.o., and Eurozet Consulting sp. z o.o. — passed resolutions implementing group layoff procedures and beginning consultations with trade unions operating in these companies. Additional cuts at Agora itself and its radio units will proceed outside formal group layoff frameworks due to smaller numbers not exceeding statutory thresholds.

The announcement, published in a stock exchange filing Tuesday morning, triggered an immediate market reaction. Agora's share price rose more than 3.4% within minutes of the 10:00 AM disclosure, climbing above 4.5% by 11:20 AM before settling around 2.7% higher at 9.04 złoty by 11:40 AM. Investors apparently welcomed the cost-cutting measures despite the human toll.

Agora currently operates nine radio stations including Radio Zet, Radio TOK FM, Antyradio, and Radio Złote Przeboje. The group owns flagship newspaper Gazeta Wyborcza and its digital counterpart Wyborcza.pl, plus portal Gazeta.pl. Its portfolio extends to Helios cinema chain, Next Film production and distribution company, outdoor advertising firm AMS, and Wydawnictwo Agora book publishing house.

The Digital Model That Failed

"The reason for conducting group layoffs is the transformation of the digital activity area of Agora Capital Group, which in its current operating model is not effective," Agora's management stated in the stock exchange communique. "In order to adapt to market realities and the financial capabilities of Agora Capital Group, a decision was made to eliminate some jobs, eliminate positions with overlapping scopes of responsibilities, and automate selected processes, which results in reduced demand for labor."

The stark assessment acknowledges what industry observers have suspected: Agora's pivot to digital journalism hasn't delivered expected returns. Traditional print advertising revenues collapsed across Polish media over the past decade. Digital subscriptions and advertising haven't fully compensated for those losses, leaving legacy media companies squeezed between rising operational costs and stagnant or declining revenues.

Gazeta.pl bears the heaviest proportional burden. Management plans to eliminate up to 63 positions by the end of February 2026, representing 25.82% of all employees as of December 2025. The cuts span operational support, sales, business support, and editorial departments. Losing more than a quarter of its workforce in two months represents a brutal restructuring for the digital portal.

Wyborcza, the company operating the print and digital editions of Gazeta Wyborcza, faces elimination of up to 60 positions through February 2026 across the same departmental areas. That represents 13.48% of Wyborcza's total staff — still substantial but less severe proportionally than Gazeta.pl's cuts.

Beyond the Headlines

The restructuring extends beyond these three subsidiaries subject to formal group layoff procedures. Agora SA itself plans to eliminate up to 20 positions in business and operational support areas. Grupa Radiowa Agory (Agora Radio Group) and Eurozet, broadcaster of Radio Zet, will collectively reduce staff by up to 10 people in operational support, sales, business support, and editorial departments.

These additional cuts don't qualify as group layoffs under Polish labor law because the numbers don't exceed statutory thresholds requiring formal procedures. However, Agora assured that all departing employees, regardless of layoff classification, can expect similar treatment. The company, GRA, and Eurozet plan to conduct reductions under conditions analogous to those negotiated with trade unions and employee councils in companies subject to group procedures.

Combined across all entities, Agora Capital Group will eliminate up to 166 employees through this restructuring wave, equaling 6.56% of total group employment as stated in the official announcement.

The Promise to Departing Workers

Agora pledged that Wyborcza, Gazeta.pl, and Eurozet Consulting will "carry out these changes with care for employees, offering them statutory severance packages and undertaking other protective and supportive measures." The specific details of compensation packages and support programs will emerge from ongoing consultations with trade unions, Employee Councils, and employee representatives.

On December 30, 2025, company management notified trade unions and Employee Councils about restructuring intentions. Appropriate employment offices also received notification following standard procedure designed to facilitate agreements defining criteria for selecting employees for layoffs and determining social package scopes.

The consultations typically extend several weeks under Polish labor law. Once negotiations conclude, Agora will publicly disclose estimated costs for planned employment reductions. These expenses will burden fourth quarter 2025 results, meaning the financial impact hits the books before actual layoffs proceed through February 2026.

The Troubling Pattern

This marks the latest chapter in Agora's ongoing struggle to stabilize its workforce and business model. The current structure of companies now facing layoffs resulted from a 2023 reorganization when Agora decided to spin off Gazeta Wyborcza and portal Gazeta.pl into separate limited liability companies. The process, which took approximately 18 months, aimed to increase autonomy for individual brands and improve their profitability.

Tuesday's announcement demonstrates that new structure requires further cost adjustments as Poland's digital media market proves harsh terrain. The restructuring targets a wide range of departments: operational support, sales, business support, and editorial teams all face cuts. That breadth signals fundamental business model problems rather than isolated departmental inefficiencies.

In June 2025, Agora announced Wyborcza would eliminate up to 49 positions related to closing its printing operations. The company transferred printing of Gazeta Wyborcza and several related magazines to Polska Press, abandoning its own Warsaw printing facility on Żerań to generate approximately 6 million złoty in annual savings. That closure resulted in nearly 50 print-related layoffs during summer 2025, with reserves of about 2.4 million złoty set aside for severance.

Earlier in 2024, group layoffs at the holding affected approximately 180 employees. Estimated reserve amounts totaled 10 million złoty then, suggesting similar scale reserves may be required for current restructuring. According to reports from Wirtualne Media, those 2024 layoffs swept away nearly all proofreading department staff, about 20 Warsaw printing plant workers, and many journalists including most of the BIQdata.pl team plus prominent reporters.

The Financial Reality

Despite restructuring turbulence, Agora's recent financial performance showed improvement in some metrics. In the first three quarters of 2025, the group achieved nearly 1.1 billion złoty in revenue, up 6.1% compared to the analogous period in 2024. EBITDA profit reached 176.8 million złoty, while EBIT totaled 41.1 million złoty. Both indicators improved compared to the same 2024 period — by 16.1% and 83.5% respectively.

However, the group still posted a 16 million złoty net loss. Net loss attributable to shareholders of the parent company reached 19.0 million złoty, versus 24.4 million złoty loss the previous year. The bottom line improvements haven't reached the threshold needed to sustain current employment levels, particularly in struggling digital operations.

Third quarter 2025 specifically showed 10.2% revenue growth to 368.6 million złoty, driven primarily by stronger performance from Helios cinemas and film distribution operations. Net loss narrowed from 11.5 million to 5.3 million złoty quarter-over-quarter. The improving trajectory hasn't prevented management from concluding that workforce reductions remain necessary to align costs with revenue realities.

The market's positive reaction to layoff announcements reflects investor perspective that Agora had grown too bloated relative to its revenue base. Wall Street and Warsaw financial districts typically reward cost discipline even when human consequences prove painful. The 4.5% share price spike mid-morning December 30 demonstrates that institutional investors view workforce reduction as financially prudent despite the toll on 166 families.

The Broader Media Landscape

Agora's struggles mirror challenges facing legacy media companies globally. Digital transformation promised to replace declining print revenues with subscription and digital advertising income. Reality proved messier. Digital advertising concentrates overwhelmingly with Google and Meta platforms. Building sufficient subscription bases to support legacy newsroom costs requires scale most individual publishers can't achieve. Automated content and AI-generated material threaten to further commoditize journalism.

Polish media face additional pressures. The domestic market limits growth potential compared to English-language publishers that can reach global audiences. Political tensions create business risks when advertisers fear association with controversial editorial positions. Economic headwinds reduce both consumer willingness to pay for subscriptions and corporate appetite for advertising spending.

Traditional media employment across developed economies has contracted dramatically over two decades. Newsroom jobs that once supported middle-class families disappeared as classified advertising migrated to Craigslist, retail ads moved online, and readership fragmented across countless digital sources. Agora's current restructuring represents one more data point in that broader trend rather than an isolated Polish phenomenon.

The automation push Agora cited as justification for cuts reflects another industry-wide pattern. AI-powered tools increasingly handle tasks previously requiring human labor: content aggregation, headline optimization, social media distribution, basic data journalism, even some article composition. Media companies face pressure to deploy these technologies to reduce costs even as journalists worry about job security and editorial quality.

What Comes Next

The restructuring proceeds through early 2026 with layoffs concluding by February 28. Agora must negotiate specific terms with unions and employee representatives, determining who faces termination and what compensation packages they receive. Those conversations typically prove contentious as employees and unions push for maximum protection while management seeks to minimize costs.

Fourth quarter 2025 financial results will reflect restructuring charges when Agora reports earnings. The magnitude depends on negotiated severance terms, but 2024's 10 million złoty reserve for 180 layoffs suggests current cuts might require 8-10 million złoty given similar per-employee costs. That expense hits profitability temporarily but theoretically improves future margins by reducing ongoing payroll.

Whether the leaner Agora proves more competitive and financially sustainable remains uncertain. Cost reduction addresses symptoms rather than underlying business model problems. Digital journalism profitability remains elusive for most publishers globally. Smaller staffs mean reduced content production capacity and potentially lower quality, which could accelerate audience erosion if readers perceive diminishing value.

The 166 people losing jobs face an uncertain Polish labor market. Journalism positions remain scarce as multiple media companies contract simultaneously. Digital marketing, communications, and sales roles offer more opportunities, but competition intensifies as displaced media workers flood those sectors. Some will transition successfully to new careers; others will struggle through extended unemployment or settle for positions paying less than their media jobs offered.

For Gazeta Wyborcza specifically, the cuts raise questions about the publication's long-term viability. The newspaper holds iconic status in Polish journalism as a voice of liberal democratic opposition since Solidarity movement days. But sentiment doesn't pay bills. If digital transformation continues faltering and losses persist, further restructuring or even closure becomes conceivable despite the brand's historical significance.
