TITLE: Exclusive: Meta planning sweeping layoffs as AI costs mount
PUBLISHED: 2026-03-14
================================================================================

NEW YORK/SAN FRANCISCO, March 13 (Reuters) - Meta is contemplating substantial layoffs that could impact 20% or more of its employees, according to three sources acquainted with the situation, as the company aims to mitigate the financial burden of its investments in artificial intelligence infrastructure and adapt to the efficiencies generated by AI-supported personnel.

A timeline for these layoffs has yet to be established, and the extent of the reductions remains uncertain, the sources indicated.

High-ranking executives have recently communicated these intentions to other senior figures within Meta, instructing them to begin strategizing on potential reductions.

"This is speculative reporting regarding hypothetical strategies," stated Meta spokesperson Andy Stone.

Should Meta proceed with the 20% reduction, it would mark the most significant layoffs since a restructuring effort late 2022 and 2023, which the company referred to as the "year of efficiency." As of December 31, the company had nearly 79,000 employees.

In November 2022, Meta laid off 11,000 workers, amounting to around 13% of its workforce at that time. Approximately four months later, another reduction of 10,000 jobs was announced.

## Zuckerberg's Emphasis on Generative AI

Throughout the past year, CEO Mark Zuckerberg has been urging Meta to intensify its efforts in generative AI. The firm has offered substantial compensation packages, some reaching hundreds of millions of dollars over a four-year period, to attract leading AI researchers to a newly formed superintelligence team.

The company has disclosed plans to invest $600 billion in constructing data centers by 2028. Additionally, Meta is reportedly investing at least $2 billion to purchase the Chinese AI startup Manus.

Zuckerberg has hinted at the efficiency improvements resulting from these investments, noting in January that he was beginning to observe "projects that previously required large teams now being completed by a single highly skilled individual."

Meta's strategy aligns with a wider trend among major U.S. corporations, especially in the tech sector, this year. In January, Amazon confirmed it would reduce its workforce by approximately 16,000 jobs. Last month, the fintech firm Block laid off nearly half of its employees, with CEO Jack Dorsey explicitly attributing the decision to the capabilities of AI tools.
